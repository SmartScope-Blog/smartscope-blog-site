      Google Gemini Complete Guide

      How to Read Gemini 3.1 Pro After GPT-5.4 (April 2026)¶

      What this article does

      Separates what is still true about Gemini 3.1 Pro from what has become outdated in its older comparison framing Explains why Google's February 2026 comparison sheet should not be treated as today's full leaderboard Repositions Gemini 3.1 Pro after GPT-5.4 and Claude Sonnet 4.6 arrived as the new comparison baseline

      The older story around Gemini 3.1 Pro was built around Google's February 2026 comparison sheet and launch messaging. That framing is now incomplete, because OpenAI's current comparison anchor is no longer GPT-5.2 or GPT-5.3-Codex. It is GPT-5.4.

      That does not make Gemini 3.1 Pro irrelevant. It does mean the right question has changed. Instead of asking, "Did Gemini win 13 out of 16 rows?", the more useful April 2026 question is:

      What still makes Gemini 3.1 Pro compelling, and which parts of the older comparison sheet are now historical rather than current?

      Short answer¶

      What you want to knowCurrent answer
      Is Gemini 3.1 Pro still Google's top model for hard tasks?Yes. Google still describes it as its most advanced model for complex tasks
      Can Google's older public comparison table still serve as today's full OpenAI/Claude/Google ranking?No. It predates GPT-5.4
      What still makes Gemini 3.1 Pro attractive?1M context, multimodal scope, Google-native agentic/search workflows, and competitive pricing
      Should Gemini 3.1 Pro still appear in a current comparison cluster?Yes, but as a current option, not as a frozen February victory table

      What Google still says today¶

      Google DeepMind's model card continues to position Gemini 3.1 Pro as Google's most advanced model for complex tasks. It supports text, images, audio, video, and entire code repositories, with 1M context and 64K output.

      The pricing also remains competitive:

      Prompt sizeInputOutput
      <= 200K tokens$2.00$12.00
      > 200K tokens$4.00$18.00

      Those numbers keep Gemini 3.1 Pro firmly in the shortlist for serious multimodal, long-context, and cost-aware work.

      Why the old comparison framing is now incomplete¶

      Google's eval methodology document makes three important things explicit:

      • non-Gemini results are generally sourced from providers' self-reported numbers
      • for Claude Opus 4.6, Sonnet 4.6, and GPT-5.2, Google defaults to maximum thinking / reasoning settings available
      • the methodology itself is dated February 2026

      That matters because the OpenAI side of the market changed after that sheet was published.

      What changed after February 2026¶

      OpenAI moved the comparison baseline to GPT-5.4¶

      OpenAI's GPT-5.4 release now publishes a new official comparison set including:

      • SWE-Bench Pro (Public): 57.7%
      • Terminal-Bench 2.0: 75.1%
      • OSWorld-Verified: 75.0%

      So any Gemini article that still treats GPT-5.3-Codex as the current OpenAI comparison baseline is behind the state of the market.

      Anthropic's side is now clearly two-layered¶

      Anthropic still positions Opus 4.6 as its top model. But Anthropic also made Sonnet 4.6 the default model in claude.ai and Claude Cowork, at $3 / $15 per million tokens.

      That means current comparisons should not reduce the Claude side to a single line item. The more realistic framing is:

      • Opus 4.6 for flagship comparisons
      • Sonnet 4.6 for practical day-to-day operation

      What still makes Gemini 3.1 Pro compelling¶

      1. Google still places it at the top of its own stack¶

      This part did not change. Gemini 3.1 Pro is still Google's complex-task flagship and still relevant for anyone who values the Google ecosystem, broad multimodality, and long-context problem solving.

      2. 1M context plus multimodal input is still a real package¶

      Gemini 3.1 Pro's appeal is not just pricing. It is the combination of:

      • 1M context
      • multimodal input
      • Google-native search and tool ecosystem alignment

      That remains a credible reason to shortlist it even after GPT-5.4 launched.

      3. Pricing is still competitive¶

      At $2 / $12 for prompts up to 200K tokens, Gemini 3.1 Pro remains cheaper than GPT-5.4 at $2.50 / $15 and clearly below Claude Opus 4.6 at $5 / $25.

      What should no longer be overclaimed¶

      1. "13 out of 16 wins" as if it were today's full market ranking¶

      That framing was always launch-specific. In April 2026, it is better treated as a historical snapshot of Google's published comparison position, not the current universal leaderboard.

      2. GPT-5.3-Codex as the current OpenAI-side reference¶

      That is no longer the clean comparison anchor. OpenAI has moved to GPT-5.4 across ChatGPT, the API, and Codex.

      3. Claude as one undifferentiated competitor¶

      That loses too much nuance now that Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6 serve different roles in practice.

      The April 2026 read on Gemini 3.1 Pro¶

      If the use case is...Gemini 3.1 Pro today
      Google-native multimodal and long-context workflowsStrong fit
      Cost-aware high-end model usageStill attractive
      Current broad model comparisonStill relevant, but must be compared against GPT-5.4-era context
      Strongest current public coding / CLI signalLess clear than GPT-5.4

      Summary¶

      • Gemini 3.1 Pro is still a real top-tier option
      • But Google's public comparison framing is still February 2026 framing
      • Any current Gemini article should shift from "did it win the launch table?" to "where does it still make sense today?"
      • In April 2026, the natural broad comparison set is GPT-5.4, Opus 4.6, Sonnet 4.6, and Gemini 3.1 Pro
